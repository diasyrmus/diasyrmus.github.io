So i picked up a clinical psychology textbook on Anxiety by S Rachman,
and immediately the value of the approach was clear. It was a learning
process and it prompted personal engagement with that learning
process. 

The relation of anxiety to fear, the ambiguities inherent in the
terms; you can be anxious to see a play, you might seek out fear on a
rollercoaster ride, but you would not seek out *anxiety* as such. How
fear is a combination of three things: physiological symptons, the
desire for flight, and the self-described experience of being
frightened, but that without the latter the presence of the other two
is not enough to identify fear. The self-description is essential and
it cannot be gauged by outside observers, even ones measuring
apparently objective physiological symptoms. That the only time the
three symptoms are evenly present is both at the moment of greatest
fear, ie prior to a parachute jump, or at the moment most distant from
it, ie a period of complete calm before or after. 

That these symptoms appear and also disappear in response to treatment desynchronously.

The relation of anxiety to fear isn't yet clear to me.
