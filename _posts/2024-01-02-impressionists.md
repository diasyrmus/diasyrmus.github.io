A good exhibition, on how these late 19th early 20th C artists captured the tempo of their age, of the city. 

> Imbued with our surroundings, our sentiments, the things of our age – Edouard Douranty (1876)

This seems to me the aim of any art. The best pieces here capture 'the things of our age'. I particularly liked a study of a woman reviewing her dance card - that dance card being a 'thing of the age', the practical tilt of the woman's head, showing her absorbed in a task of courtship, or more sensibly 'who do I want to avoid, and with whom wouldn't I mind dancing'.

You do have to wonder at the relationship of the sitters to the artist of course – Toulouse-Lautrec's patronisation of brothels for instance. But the insight into character is strong - shared comments, affection and love, people captured in the moment of elegance and being 'off duty' (see Degas' ballet dancers), or The Picnic

It might be said these pictures represent a peep behind the curtain of the manners of the age, unlaced moments, moments of preparation. 

Redon's pictures seem to me largely to be the expression of a massive ham – the phrase I want to use is 'frightful old ham'. However, Ophelia among the Flowers is powerful, with the flowers almost seeming to bloom from the subconscious or unconscious background, the fading moment of Ophelia's 'reality' of her death. It reminded me of an observation – I think it must have been in *The Rebel Angels* – of Ophelia eavesdropping. I'll need to look it up when I get back. (I'm in the RA members cafe, with a truly dreadful, pallid bag of assam tea - can it even be assam?)

Far from the best picture, Seurat's The Gleaner was very striking - the silhouette of a peasant, almost scarecrow, almost death-like, raking and picking through the harvested field. This seemed to me a very strong representation of my own psyche at the moment, scratching painfully through my harvested consciousness – that is to say, harvested by my youth, not used to the extent it should have been, put in the service of less vital, less important activities and work. It also seemed to me to be the process of psychotherapy maybe - _gleaning_ whatever can be gleaned from the decayed and partial labyrinth and hinterlands of the mind. 

I do not, incidentally, agree with Werner Herzog's comment that psychotherapy is a destructive exercise, as there are some parts of the mind that should be left dark. The mind is too vast, too strange, its magnitude too much a function of its dimensions and involutions, not to draw its maps, its routes with question marks at the end. Indeed, psychotherapy, and art generally maybe, can be seen as a way of postponing arriving at absolutes of death or philosophy. 

The exhibition itself seemed to me to be overwhelmed by the presence of a beautiful, much older French woman, probably in her early 60s, dressed elegantly in a long floral dress, whose eyes I caught directly when turning back from a sketch of fortifications outside Paris by Van Gogh.

And yesterday of course, there was that rather exciting German woman at the Genius bar at the Apple Store, taking to me about Pina Bausch, with that irrepressible german accent.

Maybe 2024 will be a year of romances, though as always whether I am ready to take advantage of those is another matter. My lack of serendipity, of letting the woman decide, has been crippling throughout my life. Though while my intellect decays, mightn't my behaviour towards myself improve? 

To revisit this theme of the beginning of the year, I have also wondered whether this intellectual collapse is also a function of how information is distributed across radically different fields. 'What should be known' or knowledge in general, was working in a much more confined space when I was growing up. it makes it easier to remember, to plug in to thinking, and the sets of related data. As well as my current lack of engagement with knowledge – by trying to answer the question before asking it, or by seeking to find out more about that which interests me – which I think is causing its own decay, and my mental decay from years of drinking, and my periods of – frankly – alcoholism – as well as all that, my mind is trying to cover an increasingly fragmentary set of fields. This might be a version of the 'digital distraction' problem of social media, phones etc.
