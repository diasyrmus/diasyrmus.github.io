What does it mean when you're asked to participate as an 'ally' in the workplace? 

I was recently asked to participate in some D&I material on a slide for 'allies' for LGBTQ+. I was automatically happy to do so. It required a photo of me pointing in a direction, which would link to a quote. I said I didn't like the boilerplate quotes, which seemed to me extremely anodyne, to which the not unreasonable response was 'ok, come up with your own.'

Fine.

Finding the right words was hard though. Immediately I started coming up against who owns empowerment. I mean, I must admit my first two attempts, which were probably my most sincerely felt were:

'If you're intolerant of LGBTQ+ experiences, people and communities, you're a massive twat' < - unhelpfully gendered swearword, but in UK English, it expresses almost exactly the type.*

and

'Really, who gives a shit how other people want to love and have sex, or how they want to identify? Mind your own business!'

However, both of these also indicated the problem, specifically in the workspace. Largely, I agree with a 'mind your own business' approach, and view that people's private lives should be allowed to be private. However, as the workplace is a community, participation in which people's livelihoods depend. It cannot be truly seen as a voluntary group, to which you can simply choose to belong or leave; doing so will often involve some degree of psychological or financial stress. 

In fact the workplace is a real test of inclusion.

1) Seeking to aggregate to oneself the specific richness, pleasure, happiness, pride, anger, etc emotions and experiences, which the outgroup have fought for. One of the draft quotes was 'diversity enriches us all, intolerance is a narrowing, impoverishment. by supporting and listening to LGBTQ+ people and communities we can all take pride in that diversity and richness'. **We can all take pride**. There is a sense of absorbing some of the power and that diversity away from the marginal group and into the power group. As a member of that group I think I would be saying, 'these things are *our* things, they are *not* for you.' Battening on to them, trying to participate in them in some way, seeking to absorb them into your power structures, is only another form of expression of the power group to which you belong. **We want what you have**

2) The perception of being in a position to *allow* people to be supported or express themselves. 'We want to listen to you' is a statement that would generate some pretty sharp retorts in some contexts, I think. It is the patronisation of those in power.

3) I have no real concern about the appearance of 'white knighting', a term which largely seems to be used by those in positions of power to criticise those attempting to speak for or assist minority groups in spaces which are resistent to them, though the term does draw down on the inherent vulnerabilities presented by both 1+2 above.

 I think there are things that need to happen to ensure that 1&2 are not at play. 
 
 Some important thing I think it needs if you're going to be used as an ally in a corporate space:
 * STRUCTURE: Is this taking place in a framework of structured thinking and action, or is it just corporate wallpaper?
 * VOICE: I think probably a place for anonymous feedback and a questionnaire about minority experiences in your workplace should ideally be a *precondition* of participation.
 * ARE YOU DOING THE WORK?: As an ally, are you trying to see how people outside the main power space communicate, the frameworks for social interaction, the language used, the possible barriers to inclusion and advancement? (If you put out an internal job ad, how do you go about ensuring people who might lack confidence because they are regularly spoken over or ignored can be enabled to be interviewed, how do you ensure in the interview that the language and mannerisms the candidate uses are understood, rather than dismissed or marked down for having a different language or communication space.) You are not doing this to learn and absorb the linguistic spaces, but to ensure they are understood as different and can be comprehended *on their own terms*, not *translated*, but ensuring the structures around them can comprehend them. This is the difference between telling, say, a shy person, 'you just need to be a bit noisier, a bit more demanding' and saying 'ok, how do I ensure this person is heard, and how do i ensure spaces where they have the confidence to express themselves'. It also means being alive to value that may not express itself like traditional value. This is not an attempt to say that every single person needs to be accommodated by structural changes, but it is something that is required for LGBTQ+ and BAME groups, and also where you perceive value in a person, but think they are not able to represent it to the right people. (Perception of value is in itself an enormously difficult area, but see again the need to engage with the frameworks and codes of other spaces on their own terms).
 
 The 'you're a twat' quote brings other considerations. The broadcast engineering world is not known for its diversity. There are many *very nice* colleagues I know, who would be mortified if they were making a colleague unhappy or uncomfortable. But casual language and I have made my own casual mistakes of expression and behaviour, that I would want to change. They probably also would feel uncomfortable talking about diversity in some of the terms I've mentioned here. (It's not just marginal groups who can feel excluded by language and concepts). Most would generally have a view 'if they can do the job i don't care what they look like or do', which is a starting point, though it may need to be recognised that 'do the job' may imply methods of commmunication or professionalism which are entirely cognate with the powerful ingroup.
 
 How best to engage with well-meaning people who won't have experienced the needs of diverse groups in the workplace before is an important question that needs considering. Telling people they need to be more tolerant seems hardly likely to generate the best response. Showing them anonymous experiences - good and bad - of the workplace they're in may be one good idea. Introducing them to people who have had made a success outside their workfplace, and said what mattered to them may help. I think most people are interested.
 
 I *do* think a clear instruction to avoid sexist jokes and conversation is necessary. You can still encounter it from time to time - doesn't need to be anything other than 'women drivers' level joking to be unacceptable.

Finally, I wanted to point out what I consider to be the genuine value of diversity in the workspace. Business speak often requests people think 'outside the box'. I remember standing on a walkway at IBC, after having been to the toilet, in which an ad for a piece of serverware had decided this would best be ornamented by a scantily clad, busty woman. I looked over IBC, the sheer white maleness of which beggars belief, and consider the constraints to development and broadcast technologies applied by a technical version of the male gaze. Progression happens from unexpected places, culturally, it often comes from outgroups, the more diversity in your workspace, the more like you are to avoid narrow thinking. increasing the salience of a diversity of approaches, codes, opinions increases the frame of reference within which you are working. This has genuine business value. 

Oh, one other 'finally' - I wanted to use the phrase LGTBTQ+ experiences, people and communities, to indicate that this sort of diversity may not be consistently located in one body. To recognise that embracing diversity also means some self-exploration of the body, sex and feelings (which is why I suspect some people fear it). The converse of not *taking* or *laying claim* to the experiences and emotions of outgroups is that if you have those experiences and feelings, or thoughts, of if you want to have them or explore them, the group is there, and by working towards more receptive and inclusive workspaces you allow a greater range of experiences and thoughts.
