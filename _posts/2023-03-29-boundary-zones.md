An LRB [article](https://www.lrb.co.uk/the-paper/v45/n07/nicola-jennings/at-the-royal-academy)(£) on the Royal Academy exhibition *Spain and the Hispanic World; Treasures from the Hispanic Society Museum and Library* reviews the collection of Archer Huntington, usually displayed at Washington Heights. 

Huntington is described as 'more of a taxonomer' than the typical European masterpiece collector:

'.. acquiring hundreds of thousands of objects to create "a broad outline without duplication" of Spanish and Hispanic art and culture.'

*a broad outline without duplication* seems like a wonderful and Borgesian phrase to describe taxonomy, and in its inverse, other forms of collection - intensive duplication of a subject, with grey areas at the boundaries where the subject is visible in other groups, sets, families, phyla.

Thoughts on this track intersected with dipping into the selection of Clifford Geertz essays in *[Local Knowledge](https://monoskop.org/images/d/d9/Geertz_Clifford_Local_Knowledge_Further_Essays_in_Interpretive_Anthropology_1983.pdf)*, specifically the essay *Fact and Law in Comparative Perspective*.

The chief enjoyment of reading Geertz is the quality of thought displayed in his writing. The control and management of categorisation and perspective to generate insight. That ability to categorise is of course the ability to draw boundaries – here 'the place of fact in the world of judgment', the distinction between 'what is right' and 'what is so' and separating western methdologies from other forms of juridical practice achieving similar ends.

That ability to know when to take distance from engaging in argument is used to represent the the dispute and thus better understand it.
