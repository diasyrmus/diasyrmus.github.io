# how to work together 1

the first of these posts will look at some initial considerations and learnings from the early stages of remote working as a team, the second will look at what a future virtual environment based on remote working may need to consider to be successful.

when our team first realised we were going to have to work from home, probably for a prolonged period, we wanted to make sure our work during this period was as effective as possible. We also wanted to make sure we retained our strong sense of team spirit. We're a tightly-bound team – I consider myself exceptionally lucky to be able to work with them – how to that maintain spirit where we weren't in the same physical space I felt was an important question.

i'd been reading a set of late lectures by the French theorist and semiologist Roland Barthes, _How to Live Together: Novelistic Simulations of Some Everyday Spaces_. There were three specific concepts in the lectures on which I'd become focused:

* _idiorhythme_: "Composed of _idios_ (particular) and _rhuthmos_ (rhythm), the word, which belongs to a religious vocabulary, refers to any community that respects each individual's own personal rhythm. "Idiorrythmy" names the lifestyle of certain monks of Mount Athos, who live alone and yet are dependent on a monastery; _at once autonomous and members of a community, solitary and integrated.._" (my italics)
* 

I'd been specifically considering these concepts with regard to voices in women's writing, and how considerations of style, composition and form could accommodate the material conditions of women's writing (and potentially that of other voices not in possession of enough social power to control for their own space and time). In particular this applied to the capacity for _interruption_, the lack of control of your environment, compared to the male writer in his shed or study, the priority demands of children, and other necessities, which not being in control of your space brings. Fundamentally, what are the approaches that allow work to be achieved in a contended environment.

These seemed like they would be potential issues in lockdown as well, as, to take one example, childcare was likely to present significant requirements for different patterns of working.

So, it had to be atemporal: anything relying on chat, or Direct Messaging, **out**. Nothing to stop those chats happening of course, but you shouldn't be relying on this mechanism to get things done. (I'm dubious generally about the value of 'read me NOW' DM-ing in the workspace - it's distracting and rarely necessary. I'd much rather use it for informal chat - and the slack and teams approach, where effectively a message is **left** and a notification appears is a better approach, allowing for chat, but also for questions to be asked and answered much later.

We're taught emails are bad - your inbox shouldn't be your to do list (correct, but oh so hard). Counterexamples like Slack, and now Microsoft Teams have tried to find other solutions to this where you **join** a space rather than being included in a thread or cc.
Emails accumulate on top of useful information. More recent stuff drowns out the past regardless of the importance of the information contained in there or the actions described.

rollback – GitHub and jira.

* short tasks, contributing to a dossier
* central platforms, with spaces where information can meaningful go, be retrieved and contributed to (effectively these spaces represent 'state')
* processes with clear gates (the equivalent of holding up to ensure no rollback)
