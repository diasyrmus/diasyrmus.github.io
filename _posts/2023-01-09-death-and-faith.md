The other day, sitting in a comfortable armchair and reading, I had a panic attack. The pressure the possibility of death places on the decisions we make, the way committing to a project or programme of thinking excludes other possibilities, and the fact those projects may never reach reach fruition (language learning, a novel, research, leaving aside entirely the huge other zone of emotional commitments) produced a microscopically short-term loop in the mind, a jittery paralysis. It feels like you have only been averting your eyes. 

This happens to me once or twice a year, but is present in some aspect all the time. It's a psychological problem.

I was reading a poem - not one I was enormously enjoying - when I came across a description that brought back erc 
Two girls break into a swimming pool:

> This we may presume: how they got in after hours, drunk,  
> 
> daring each other to swim beneath the bluepgreen tar that
> 
> each night tucked the water up; how the first girl panicked
> 
> halfway under, tried to surface where the surface wasn't;
> 
> and the second, seeing the bump in the plastic, went in
> 
> to end up the same way, a second bump, trying to push off
> 
> the suck of the whole pool against the heavy sheet.

As I say the poem itself didn't win me over - the pattern interference an intereference of metaphor and descriptive analogy that kept on missing the mark, giving it a monotonous feeling. 

But I've continued to think about this passage and about the presence of death, running side by side with life, where it feels at any moment like the two paths might touch. It reminds me of a vividly written description of the Battle of the White Mountain in Cecily Veronica Wedgwood's etc (end of protestantism, end of magic, massive european death):

> 

Two more examples. I get hit by a car while cycling home four winters ago, the car drives off and leaves me lying by the side of the road  very dazed. after some time when i try to stand up i almost vomit from the pain and have to lie down again. A woman comes along and checks how i am and ludicrously i say i'll be ok. she's not convinced and tries to stay (thank you, wherever you are!) but i force her away. 

Eventually I get up and, stopping every few hundred metres or so because of the pain, walk the few miles back to my flat. (I was in shock but this Bert Trautman sort of performance is clearly absurd.) I want to go to bed but recognise i must get to hospital. I do and am told I've broken my collar bone, some bones in my elbow and the scaphoid at the base of my hand. I'm patched up and given some tramadol. This is great. I've had tramadol before and there's no pain it can't soothe and it provides a sort of immobile elation that's very enjoyable. I go to a friend's who's said she'll put me up for a couple of days and watch Inspector Montalbano. On the tramadol it is like watching 2001: A Space Odyssey. 

That night, lying awkwardly in my bed, shifting to avoid the pain, my fatigue and the pills mean I keep swan diving to increasing depths towards sleep before jolting back out with increasingly violent jactitation, and that sharp additional kick the heart gives because it thinks you are dying. Finally, I dive and dive further than I can ever recall. I am aware how deep it is, but the descent into the darkness is also something pleasant, restful, there is a sort of passive fear but the process is resistless. I am in the same place as under the covers in the school swimming pool. It is the same darkness. I awake with an almighty body spasming jolt and am covered in sweat and gasping. 

 a few years before i went to hospital and they found a large tumour in my chest - "about the size of a small football" the South African doctor said. They couldn't tell much about it from the initial scans, and were booking me in for an emergency MRI in the next week or so. during that time i was fairly convinced i was going to die, or at least realised it was a serious possibility. this then was the actual zone of death. what did i do? i continued to go to work. I continued to train the people it was my job to train. i did not go and do a load of things that i wanted to do. i couldn't even think of the things i wanted to do. two things characterised this period. one, a saturating inability to attend to people or things clearly, as if suffering from dull low-level shock, compensated for by very 

the other was an almost idle indifference. now it was here there didn't seem much to be done about it, other than go through it. 

i had the scan, and the tumour turned out to be benign rather than malignant. large, but benign. the surgery to remove it was major but i would be ok apart from a permanent loss of sensation on the right side of my torso, an acceptable more minor form of death. 

During the later stages of a considerable recuperation period I used to go to the same library with the armchairs and read all day. At one point, with no real intent, I got into that rather cosy fireside genre of early 20th C Christian apologetics. Chesterton, CS Lewis. I think I had also been reading Karamazov so it probably wasn't quite as random as it seems now. i did and do find much of _Orthodoxy_ to be quite enjoyable and convincing, it a literary sort of way, tho a sententious coziness 

During this I became vividly aware with how much assurance i could approach the world and my life if i believed in God. If I had faith. As I say this was vividly represented, I guess almost a vision - two huge wheeling cosmic systems, incommensurate (actually now i think about it they were actually a bit like the black holes in geometry wars but nm) - to select one would necessarily collapse entirely the possibility of the other one - but separated by only a small gap. I only needed to take what at that point felt a like small step, and have faith, to exist in a more fruitful world, existential, ontological and epistemological certainty (doubt, yes, but doubt is different when it works from faith than when it is the sceptical nature of the universe). 

At that point it felt like the only requirement was to believe in the resurrection of Christ. It felt to me not possible to be a serious Christian without believing that. 

And as I couldn't believe that I stepped back. Avoided committing. I guess it's always there, so it hasn't collapsed as a possibility, but it occurs to me that the error of Pascal's Wager, or the policy of the old Emperors, is not that it squeaks you through death, but that it fails to provide you for life. 

So that wasn't a life-changing experience. And all I got out of it.
