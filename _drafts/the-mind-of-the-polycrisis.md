When I saw Adam Tooze talk, I think it must have been about the time *Crashed* was published, he described a problem he had with publishing the book. He couldn't finish it. It was originally going to be about the GFC and the immediate response, largely seen as successful by the Fed, with some failures in the Eurozone. In the time he took to write it, Brexit happened, and Donald Trump got elected. This indicated to Tooze, as for many others, that a social reaction had taken place, against the globalised system that had brought the world's finances to the brink, where banks had taken the risks and accrued the benefits, and people, society, had paid the price, without anything like a proportionate bail out.

This may not have been realised in those terms, but the reaction was clear.

So he added the Aftershocks chapter to cover Trump, Ukraine, Greece, Hungary and Brexit.

In the talk, he confessed it had taken the intervention of his editor to stop him adding chapters on Turkey and Syria – he ended up [blogging these](https://adamtooze.com/?s=framing+crashed) as a series and as far as I can tell this was pretty much the start of him publicly becoming a 'historian of the current moment'.

Maybe that fear is history itself, the ability to come to a point of judgment, of argument. He seems oddly vulnerable to criticism (Monbiot, Anderson). It is totalising.
