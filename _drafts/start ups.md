a recent twitter exchange reminded me of one of those strands of thought that you revisit periodically, without ever really driving through with evidence or data. you sort of assume that everyone else sees what's obvious. [cut this bit out]

that is that the business models of much of the new world around us are fundamentally unstable and based on growth and a lifecycle. 

the options at the end of that lifecycle are to extend, unbundle, bundle or sunset the product, while making sure you've got offers across your portfolio that make up for the stagnation of revenue growth. 

the other option is of course to pivot – take existing capabilities and address them to a new area without any existing competition.

at its extreme, the dynamics of this world are monopoly, being bought out, or bust; these the three outcomes of start-ups.

profitable, stable business is a distant fourth in this, though it does happen. but dev-ops models that never quite transition to full operational resilience and hygiene, and the continuing existance of a product mentality, seems likely to result in a continuous destabilisation. 

One way to say this is that these businesses, so besotted by finding market fit, never actually work out how to manage their costs (see twitter thread on consultants coming in)

there are various classic examples cited: fujifilms move into plastics. but the great recent example is Netflix.
